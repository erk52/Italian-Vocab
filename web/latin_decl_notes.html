  • Cases:
  • Nominative - The subject of the sentence
  • Genative - Showing possession
  • Accusative - The direct object of the sentence. Also some prepositions involving motion towards an object.
  • Dative - The indirect object
  • Ablative - The means or instrument of the action, as well as prepositions involing motion away from an object.
The man gives the woman a rose. Vir feminae rosam dat. The man hits the boy with a stick. Vir puerum baculo pulsat.
Latin is a highly inflected language. This means that the role of a particular noun in the sentence is marked by changes to its ending, rather than by word order or prepositions as in English. There are five main cases in Latin, (plus the rarely seen Locative and Vocative) and different groups of nouns reflect these cases differently. Latin nouns are divided into five groups, called Declensions, based on how their endings change to reflect case and number. The table above briefly describes the function of each case, and there are tabs devoted to showing how nouns from each group change to reflect these cases.
  • Singular:
  • Nom - femina (-a) / feminae (-ae)
  • Gen - feminae (-ae) / feminarum (-arum)
  • Acc - feminam (-am) / feminas (-as)
  • Dat - feminae (-ae) / feminis (-is)
  • Abl - femina (-a) / feminis (-is)
Nouns of the first declension end in -a in the nominative singular, and -ae in the genative singular. Most first declension nouns are feminine. However, there are some masculine nouns (usually professions), such as nauta, agricola, poeta.
  • Masculine:
  • Nom: amicus (-us) / amici (-i)
  • Gen: amici (-i) / amicorum (-orum)
  • Acc: amicum (-um) / amicos (-os)
  • Dat: amico (-o) / amicis (-is)
  • Abl: amico (-o) / amicis (-is)
  • Neuter:
  • Nom: oppidum (-um) / oppida (-a)
  • Gen: oppidi (-i) / oppidorum (-orum)
  • Acc: oppidum (-um) / oppida (-a)
  • Dat: oppido (-o) / oppidis (-is)
  • Abl: oppido (-o) / oppidis (-is)
  • Masculine, -r ending:
  • Nom: vir / viri (-i)
  • Gen: viri (-i) / virorum (-orum)
  • Acc: virum (-um) / viros (-os)
  • Dat: viro (-o) / viris (-is)
  • Abl: viro (-o) / viris (-is)
Second declension nouns are either masculine or neuter. Neuter nouns end in -um in the nominative singular. Masculine nouns either end in -us, as in dominus, or in -r, as in puer.
  • Ovis
  • Nom: ovis (-is) / amici (-i)
  • Gen: amici (-i) / amicorum (-orum)
  • Acc: amicum (-um) / amicos (-os)
  • Dat: amico (-o) / amicis (-is)
  • Abl: amico (-o) / amicis (-is)
  • Neuter:
  • Nom: oppidum (-um) / oppida (-a)
  • Gen: oppidi (-i) / oppidorum (-orum)
  • Acc: oppidum (-um) / oppida (-a)
  • Dat: oppido (-o) / oppidis (-is)
  • Abl: oppido (-o) / oppidis (-is)
  • Masculine, -r ending:
  • Nom: vir / viri (-i)
  • Gen: viri (-i) / virorum (-orum)
  • Acc: virum (-um) / viros (-os)
  • Dat: viro (-o) / viris (-is)
  • Abl: viro (-o) / viris (-is)

Nouns of the fourth declension are weird. They end in -us, but don't confuse them for second declension nouns. That's why you need to look at the genetive form as well to know which declension a noun belongs to.
